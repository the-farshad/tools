24 July 1923

Treaty of Lausanne

Replaced Sèvres; made no provision for a Kurdish state

Sèvres undone

On 24 July 1923, the Treaty of Lausanne was signed between the Allied Powers and the new Republic of Turkey, replacing the unratified Treaty of Sèvres. Lausanne defined the modern borders of Turkey and made no provision for a Kurdish state or autonomy. Kurdistan was definitively split among Turkey, Iraq (then a British mandate), Syria (a French mandate), and Iran.

Lausanne is widely cited in Kurdish political discourse as the moment a Kurdish state went, formally, from possible to deferred.
