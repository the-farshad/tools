10 August 1920

Treaty of Sèvres

Articles 62-64 promised an autonomous Kurdish region

A promise on paper

On 10 August 1920, the Allied Powers and the Ottoman Empire signed the Treaty of Sèvres at the close of the First World War. Articles 62, 63, and 64 provided for an autonomous Kurdish region in southeastern Anatolia, with the possibility of full independence after one year if the Kurdish population so wished and the League of Nations agreed.

The treaty was never ratified. The rise of Mustafa Kemal's Turkish national movement made it dead-letter; it was replaced three years later by the Treaty of Lausanne, which dropped any reference to Kurdish autonomy.

A promise the Powers wrote and then forgot.
